# Depth Lenses

Use these to add intellectual depth beyond surface-level advice. Pick 1-2 per piece, not all.

## Philosophy Lenses

**Stoicism** — Focus on controllable vs uncontrollable

- Use when: Setbacks, uncertainty, career pressure
- Key move: "What can you actually control here? Your skills, your effort, your response."
- Sources: Marcus Aurelius (Meditations), Epictetus

**Existentialism** — Choice, freedom, identity

- Use when: Career transitions, life direction, quarter-life/mid-life questions
- Key move: "The anxiety isn't about the job. It's about who you become."
- Sources: Sartre, Camus

**Eastern (Dao/Yin-Yang)** — Flow, balance, long-term thinking

- Use when: Strategy, patience, relationship dynamics
- Key move: "Water doesn't fight the rock. It flows around it."
- Sources: Laozi (Dao De Jing), Sunzi

## Sociology Lenses

**Cultural Capital** (Bourdieu) — Hidden advantages from background

- Use when: Explaining "unfair" advantages, networking, education gaps
- Key move: "It's not that they're smarter. They grew up speaking the language of power."

**Group Behavior** (Le Bon, Cialdini) — Herding, social proof, polarization

- Use when: Trend analysis, market prediction, opinion formation
- Key move: "Everyone's saying X, which is exactly why I'm suspicious."

**Generational** — Different cohorts, different survival strategies

- Use when: Workplace culture, consumer behavior, value clashes
- Key move: "Gen Z isn't disloyal. They're rational actors in a different economy."

## Psychology Lenses

**Loss Aversion** (Kahneman) — People fear losing more than they desire gaining

- Use when: Persuading action, urgency, risk framing
- Key move: "You're not missing an opportunity. You're actively losing $X every month you wait."
- Flip: Frame inaction as loss, not action as gain

**Identity & Belonging** (Tajfel) — People act to protect their in-group identity

- Use when: Community building, us-vs-them narratives, career identity
- Key move: "If you call yourself a founder but you've never done this, are you really one?"
- Caution: Powerful but can be manipulative — use to include, not exclude

**Cognitive Load** (Sweller) — Overwhelmed brains shut down

- Use when: Explaining complex ideas, tutorials, frameworks
- Key move: One concept per section. If you need a glossary, you've already lost.
- Technique: Analogy first, definition second, never the reverse

**Emotional Triggers** — The 6 high-share emotions (Berger & Milkman)

- High arousal positive: Awe, excitement, humor → most shareable
- High arousal negative: Anger, anxiety → shareable but risky
- Low arousal: Sadness → low shares unless paired with hope
- Key move: Identify which emotion each section triggers. No section should be emotionally neutral.

**Curiosity Gap** (Loewenstein) — The gap between what we know and what we want to know

- Use when: Hook design, section transitions, cliffhangers
- Key move: Open a question, delay the answer. "I made one change. I'll tell you what it was — but first..."

## Communication Lenses

**Clarity Hierarchy** — Say it simple, then go deep

- Rule: If a 15-year-old can't understand your first paragraph, rewrite it
- Structure: Claim → proof → nuance (not nuance → nuance → nuance → claim)
- Test: Cover the bottom half of your draft. Does the top half standalone?

**Story > Argument** — Narratives persuade more than logic

- Use when: You catch yourself listing reasons ("because X, because Y, because Z")
- Key move: Replace the argument with a single story that embodies all the reasons
- "Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass." — Chekhov

**Conversation, Not Lecture** — Write like you're talking to one person

- Signals you're lecturing: No "you", no questions, no self-doubt, no humor
- Signals you're conversing: Direct address, rhetorical questions, asides, admissions
- Test: Count the "you"s in your draft. If < 3 per 500 words, you're lecturing.

## Viral Science Lenses

**Social Currency** (Berger) — People share things that make them look good

- Types: Insider knowledge, contrarian takes, humor, being "first"
- Key move: Write something the reader can share to signal "I'm smart / I'm in the know"
- Test: Would sharing this make the reader look good to their followers?

**Practical Value** — Useful content gets saved and forwarded

- Key move: Include at least one thing the reader can DO today
- Format: The more specific the actionable step, the more saves you get
- "Save this for later" triggers: numbered lists, templates, formulas, checklists

**Triggers & Top-of-Mind** — Link your topic to everyday cues

- Key move: Associate your idea with something people encounter daily
- Example: "Every time you open your email, remember this framework"
- Why: Content tied to daily triggers gets recalled and reshared organically

## Data Techniques

**First-hand** (n=1 but deep):

- Your own experience, interviews, experiments
- Strength: Authenticity, specificity
- "I tried this for 30 days. Here's what actually happened."

**Second-hand** (scaled credibility):

- Academic papers, industry reports, public data
- Always cite source, always acknowledge limitations
- "McKinsey says X — but here's why that might not apply to solo founders."

**Contrast data**: "A is X% higher than B" > "A is Y"
**Personalize data**: "That means if you earn $50K, you're losing..."

## Cross-Domain Analogy

Map unfamiliar → familiar:

| Concept       | Analogy                                          |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Investing     | Planting trees (time, patience, compound growth) |
| Career growth | RPG leveling (skill trees, XP, boss fights)      |
| Relationships | Bank accounts (deposits, withdrawals, interest)  |
| Startups      | Expeditions (maps, gear, unknown risks)          |
| AI prompting  | Talking to a brilliant but literal intern        |

**Steps to build good analogies:**

1. Identify the core abstract concept
2. Find a domain your audience knows well
3. Map the structural relationships (not surface features)
4. Acknowledge where the analogy breaks down (builds trust)

## Layered Writing

- **Surface**: Stories, examples, scenes → accessible to everyone
- **Middle**: Methods, frameworks, actionable steps → for practitioners
- **Deep**: Theory, philosophy, first principles → for thinkers

Not every piece needs all three layers. Match depth to platform and audience.
